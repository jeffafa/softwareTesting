This workshop is about proving things by induction, and about the connection between recursion and induction. More precisely, this workshop is about how you prove things by induction on data-types defined by recursion. Background reading for this topic is The Haskell Road, chapter 7.

For further instruction on this topic from YouTube, have a look at this Khan Academy video.

The simplest case of proof by (mathematical) induction is induction on the natural numbers. The task is to prove that a property PP holds of all natural number. The proof has two steps:

  1. Show that the property PP holds for the number 00.

  2. Show that if the property PP holds for the number nn, then it will also hold for n+1n+1.

In the second case, the assumption that PP holds for nn is called the induction hypothesis. The best way to learn proof by induction is by practice. Here we go. (And again: watch the video mentioned above.)

  • Prove by induction that it holds for all natural numbers nn that

    1+2+⋯+n=n(n+1)2.1+2+⋯+n=n(n+1)2.

  • Prove by induction that it holds for all natural numbers nn that

    12+22+⋯+n2=n(n+1)(2n+1)6.12+22+⋯+n2=n(n+1)(2n+1)6.

  • Prove by induction that it holds for all natural numbers nn that

    13+23+⋯+n3=(n(n+1)2)2.13+23+⋯+n3=(n(n+1)2)2.

  • Prove by induction that if AA is a finite set with |A|=n|A|=n, then |P(A)|=2n|P(A)|=2n.

  • A permutation of a list is a reordering of the members of a list. Here is a Haskell implementation:

> perms :: [a] ->[[a]]
> perms [] = [[]]
> perms (x:xs) = concat (map (insrt x) (perms xs)) where
>   insrt x [] = [[x]]
>   insrt x (y:ys) = (x:y:ys) : map (y:) (insrt x ys)

Find a formula (closed form) for the number of permutations of a list of nn distinct objects, and prove your guess by induction.

  • Prove by induction that it holds for all natural numbers nn that
    32n+3+2n is divisible by 7.32n+3+2n is divisible by 7.

A useful generalisation of mathematical induction on the natural numbers is structural induction, where we wish to prove that some property PP holds of all members of a recursively defined datatype such as trees, lists or formulas.

  • Recall the definition of formulas of propositional logic:

    ϕ::=p∣(¬ϕ)∣(ϕ∧ϕ)∣(ϕ∨ϕ)∣(ϕ→ϕ)∣(ϕ↔ϕ)ϕ::=p∣(¬ϕ)∣(ϕ∧ϕ)∣(ϕ∨ϕ)∣(ϕ→ϕ)∣(ϕ↔ϕ)
    Give a recursive definition of the number of occurrences of connectives in a propositional formula.

  • Give a recursive definition of the number of occurrences of atoms (atomic subformulas) in a formula of propositional logic.

  • Let S(ϕ)S(ϕ) be the number of occurrences of subformulas of a propositional formula ϕϕ, let A(ϕ)A(ϕ) be the number of atoms of ϕϕ, and let C(ϕ)C(ϕ) be the number of connectives of ϕϕ. Prove by structural induction:

    S(ϕ)=A(ϕ)+C(ϕ), for all propositional formulas ϕ.S(ϕ)=A(ϕ)+C(ϕ), for all propositionalformulas ϕ.

Consider the following definition of binary trees:

> data Btree a = Leaf a | Node (Btree a) (Btree a) deriving (Eq,Show)

The depth of a binary tree is given by:

> depth :: Btree a -> Int
> depth (Leaf _) = 0 
> depth (Node t1 t2) = max (depth t1) (depth t2) + 1

  • What is the minimum number of internal nodes (non leaf nodes) that can occur in a binary tree of depth nn? First guess, by looking at examples. Next prove your guess by induction.

  • What is the minimum number of leaf nodes that can occur in a binary tree of depth nn? First guess, by looking at examples. Next prove your guess by induction.

  • What is the maximum number of leaf nodes that can occur in a binary tree of depth nn. First guess, by looking at examples. Next prove your guess by induction.

  • Consider the following program for merging two lists:

> merge :: Ord a => [a] -> [a] -> [a]
> merge xs [] = xs
> merge [] ys = ys
> merge (x:xs) (y:ys) = if x <= y 
>                          then x : merge xs (y:ys) 
>                          else y : merge (x:xs) ys

Show with induction that if xs and ys are finite and sorted, then merge xs ys is sorted.
